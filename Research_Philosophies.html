Research Philosophies & Principles Quiz

20 core practice questions based on lecture notes. Preparation for the Final Exam.

1. Which of the following best defines "research" according to Oxford Languages and standard methodologies?

Explanation: Research is defined as a systematic process of inquiry aimed at discovering, interpreting, and revising facts using data, evidence, and critical analysis.

2. In defining "Research Quality", the highest quality is achieved when the biggest impact is met with which of the following combinations?

Explanation: Research gain maps Output against Input & Process. Quality research maximizes impact while keeping time short, the approach simple, and costs/resources low.

3. What is the main purpose of a "Research Methodology"?

Explanation: Methodology describes how a researcher intends to carry out their study to solve problems and ensure valid, reliable results.

4. Which of the following lists the first three chronological steps in conducting research?

Explanation: The research flow sequence progresses through: 1. Plan, 2. Execute, 3. Analyze, 4. Write and Publish, 5. Protect, 6. Develop, 7. Commercialize.

5. Saunders' Research Onion model consists of how many layers that must be peeled back to build a methodology?

Explanation: The onion has 6 layers: Philosophy, Approach, Strategy, Choices, Time Horizon, and Techniques & Procedures.

6. Which research component explicitly defines "how we can obtain knowledge and come to understand things"?

Explanation: Ontology deals with the nature of reality (what we know), while epistemology focuses on the methods of obtaining knowledge (how we know).

7. A researcher believes reality is objective, measurable, and independent of the observer. Which philosophy are they adhering to?

Explanation: Positivism is rooted in objective observation, statistical hypothesis testing, and empirical evidence completely detached from personal opinions.

8. Which philosophy holds that reality is subjective and socially constructed, requiring the researcher to look at thoughts, ideas, and socio-cultural backdrops?

Explanation: Interpretivism values subjective experiences and views reality as constructed by individuals through social interactions, acting as the opposite extreme of Positivism.

9. What is the core defining trait of a "Pragmatism" philosophy?

Explanation: Pragmatism values utility; instead of committing exclusively to one rigid philosophy, it highlights using the best tools to practically solve the core problem.

10. A project starts directly with an existing theory and builds or tests it through empirical research. What research approach is this?

Explanation: Deductive reasoning begins with a foundational theory and works downward to test it, whereas inductive reasoning generates new theories upwards from raw observations.

11. Quantitative research most commonly maps to a deductive approach, while qualitative research typically reflects which approach?

Explanation: Inductive approaches are usually paired with qualitative research to observe themes and build up new theories from non-numerical textual/visual elements.

12. Which research strategy relies heavily on "manipulating the independent variable to observe changes in the dependent variable"?

Explanation: Experimental research explicitly measures causal relationships by actively altering independent variables under controlled baselines.

13. What strategy is best described by the phrase: "letting the data speak for itself" to inform the development of a brand new model?

Explanation: Grounded theory lets current collected data uncover its own patterns naturally, leading directly into inductive model formulation.

14. If a researcher studies a single subject intensely within its real-world context to gain an in-depth understanding, what strategy is being deployed?

Explanation: A case study focuses on detailed evaluation of a singular target subject deeply situated within real constraints.

15. In Saunders' onion, what is the core operational distinction between a "Cross-sectional" and a "Longitudinal" time horizon?

Explanation: Time horizon tracks data timeline limits: cross-sectional captures a distinct temporary moment; longitudinal looks at patterns unfolding over extended periods.

16. Under Data Science-specific concerns, which ethical parameter asks: "Are the algorithms biased?"

Explanation: Fairness checks for system algorithms skewing or demonstrating bias; Transparency determines if decision models can be reasonably explained.

17. Which fundamental research ethical principle demands avoiding careless errors, negligence, and keeping careful records of activities?

Explanation: Carefulness focuses directly on eliminating negligence, critically vetting your peers' work, and maintaining meticulous administrative logs.

18. What form of severe research misconduct involves "making up data or results and recording or reporting them"?

Explanation: Fabrication is defined strictly as inventing or creating entirely fake data structures out of thin air.

19. If a researcher alters instrumentation settings, changes records, or omits true results so that the research is inaccurately represented, they committed:

Explanation: Falsification involves manipulating physical material setups or omitting/distorting data points to engineer a false impression.

20. According to the guidelines, which item is explicitly excluded from the legal definition of "Research Misconduct"?

Explanation: The Office of Research Integrity specifies that research misconduct does not include honest error or genuine differences of opinion among experts.
