Question 1
Which branch of statistics allows a researcher to draw conclusions about a population on the basis of a sample's descriptive statistics?
Explanation: Inferential statistics acts as the bridge spanning the gap between known data (samples) and unknowable populations.
Question 2
According to the "Mnemonic Anchor" rules of data source and measurement, which pairing is completely correct?
Explanation: Remember the anchor rule: PP (Parameters come from Populations) and SS (Statistics come from Samples).
Question 3
Why can a calculated sample statistic be thought of and modeled as a random variable?
Explanation: Different random samples yield different statistics. This basic reality is called sampling variability, making the statistic act as a random variable.
Question 4
Which of the following describes the theoretical sampling distribution of a statistic?
Explanation: A sampling distribution maps all possible sample values of that specific size along with their probabilities of occurring.
Question 5
What are the three steps involved in a single iteration of the cyclical Simulation Loop used to imitate repeated sampling?
Explanation: Simulation involves repeatedly extracting an SRS, computing/recording the statistic, and replacing the items back into the pool.
Question 6
When an estimator is described as an "unbiased estimator," what does this imply about the center of its sampling distribution?
Explanation: An unbiased estimator means our "aim is true" because the average of all possible sample statistics aligns with the true target parameter.
Question 7
What major advantage do larger sample sizes (e.g., n = 1000 vs. n = 100) offer in terms of evaluating trust?
Explanation: Larger samples shrink the variability multiplier, focusing the lens and making it highly probable to obtain a sample statistic close to the true parameter.
Question 8
To get an ideal, trustworthy estimate of a population target, what properties are desired within the diagnostic target matrix?
Explanation: "No Bias, Low Variability" ensures your hits cluster tight and land directly on the true bullseye target.
Question 9
Which type of data and tracking statistic are paired together when exploring categorical variable questions like "What percentage?"
Explanation: Categorical tracking asks about success/failure rates, shifting our analysis path over to Sample Proportions (p̂).
Question 10
What formula determines the standard deviation (spread) of the sampling distribution for a sample proportion (p̂)?
Explanation: The anatomy of spread for categorical data states that standard deviation equals √[ p(1 - p) / n ].
Question 11
At what value of the true population parameter (p) is the standard deviation variance of a sample proportion maximized?
Explanation: Inherent variance in the population numerator reaches its mathematical maximum when p = 0.5.
Question 12
What two constraints must be fulfilled to satisfy "The Normal Condition" before running normal distribution metrics for a proportion?
Explanation: The large-enough checklist ensures you expect a minimum of 10 successes (np) and 10 failures (n(1-p)).
Question 13
What constraint does the "10% Condition" impose on our random sampling process without replacement?
Explanation: To safely maintain conditions of independence when sampling without replacement, the sample size can be at most 10% of the total population.
Question 14
The sampling distribution of a sample mean (x̄) is centered precisely around which value?
Explanation: Quantitatively tracking quantitative data shows that the mean of the sampling distribution of x̄ is equal to μ.
Question 15
What mathematical expression determines the standard deviation of the sampling distribution of a sample mean (x̄)?
Explanation: The spread decreases by a factor equal to the square root of your sample size, written as σ / √n.
Question 16
How does the total spread of a sample mean's sampling distribution compare to the spread of its original, raw source population?
Explanation: Because n resides in the denominator of the standard deviation formula, individual extreme values average out, making the sample distribution narrower.
Question 17
In the height case study of young women (N(64.5, 2.5)), the probability of a single individual exceeding 66.5 inches is 21%. Why does the probability of an SRS of 10 women averaging over 66.5 inches shrink to less than 1%?
Explanation: The "shrinking shadow of variance" dictates that while one individual can easily be an extreme outlier, an average of 10 items pulling together is very unlikely to stay extreme.
Question 18
What fundamental law states that when a sample size is large enough, the sampling distribution of means will be very close to Normal, regardless of the population's shape?
Explanation: The Central Limit Theorem guarantees a convergence to a Normal shape for sample means as sample size increases, even if the parent distribution is skewed, bimodal, or chaotic.
Question 19
What is the shape condition needed to apply standard normal probability calculations to sample means (x̄) if the sample size is small?
Explanation: If sample sizes are small, the sampling distribution will only be Normal if the parent source population itself is Normal from the start.
Question 20
When looking at the three distinct distributions in statistical workflows, what does a "Distribution of Sample Data" illustrate?
Explanation: Distinguish carefully: Population tracking is all individuals; Sampling distribution is statistics from all possible samples; and a Sample Data distribution tracks individuals within just one single sample.
